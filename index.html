Patterns of Migration

Providing earlier and more accurate warnings of displacement is key to improving global preparedeness to events of forced migration. Researchers at Georgetown University and York University have developed a predictive model to determine how perception, buzz, and event variables gleaned from Big Data text mining techniques influence individual's perceived threat levels, thereby providing insight into their preferences and actions in the face of potential displacement.

Research Methods

We use local print and social media, specifically Twitter, to identify the changing dynamics of events and perceptions that may directly or indirectly have triggered displacement in and from Iraq over the last few years. The newspaper and print data are part of an archive of more than 700 million publicly available open-source media articles that has been actively compiled at Georgetown University since 2006 [Singh & Pemmaraju, 2017]. We also use over 1.5 billion tweets in English and Arabic from organizations and individuals that regularly post on developments in Iraq and related to relevant hashtags, including ones associated with extremist groups in the region.

Project Team

Research Findings

Since 2014 our interdisciplinary team has published results about forced migration, algorithms for extracting signals from unstructured data, and our project methodology. The project was founded in collaboration with researchers at Georgetown University, York University, and Lawrence Livermore Laboratory with support from the National Science Foundation, the Georgetown University Massive Data Institute, the Social Sciences and Humanities Research Council of Canada, and the Georgetown Institute for the Study of Migration.

Project Overview

This project aims to develop methods and approaches for using big data in conjunction with traditional survey data to understand and eventually forecast mass movement of people who are forced to migrate. Our initial case study is Iraq and Syria. The site will be used to highlight both the theoretical and data driven methodologies that our project team uses, as we make progress.
